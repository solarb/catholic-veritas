# Claim Classification

## Catholic Veritas

This document establishes the official framework for classifying every claim made throughout Catholic Veritas. It applies to theology, Scripture, Church history, apologetics, biblical studies, historical events, archaeology, original languages, and scholarly interpretation.

Its purpose is to ensure that contributors never present speculation as certainty or opinion as defined doctrine. Readers should always be able to distinguish clearly between revealed truth, defined doctrine, authoritative teaching, theological opinion, historical evidence, historical probability, scholarly theory, and speculation.

This framework applies equally to human contributors and AI contributors. The credibility of Catholic Veritas depends upon maintaining these distinctions consistently and honestly.

## Introduction

Responsible Catholic apologetics requires more than correct conclusions. It also requires accuracy about the level of certainty attached to a claim.

Some claims are matters of Divine Revelation and therefore belong to the highest level of certainty. Some are taught authoritatively by the Church without ambiguity. Others are historically well supported but not doctrinal. Still others are theological interpretations, scholarly judgments, or plausible reconstructions that should not be presented with the same force as dogma or direct evidence.

If these levels are not distinguished, readers can be misled in multiple ways:

- theological opinion may be mistaken for binding doctrine
- historical probability may be mistaken for established fact
- scholarly theory may be mistaken for settled consensus
- apologetic argument may be mistaken for revealed truth

Catholic Veritas must therefore classify claims carefully so that the strength of the wording matches the strength of the evidence and authority behind the claim.

## Theological Classification

The following categories should be used when classifying theological claims.

### Divine Revelation

This category refers to what has been revealed by God in Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition as part of the one Deposit of Faith.

These claims stand at the highest level of authority. No lower category may override them.

### Defined Dogma

This category refers to truths definitively taught by the Church as divinely revealed and binding upon the faithful.

These claims may be described with the highest level of theological certainty within Catholic teaching.

### Definitive Catholic Teaching

This category refers to teachings held definitively by the Church, even where the classification involves distinctions in how the teaching is proposed.

These teachings are not presented as optional opinion. They should be expressed with clarity and firmness appropriate to their level.

### Ordinary Magisterial Teaching

This category refers to authentic teaching of the Church that is authoritative and should be received seriously, even where the highest form of dogmatic definition is not involved.

These teachings should not be reduced to mere private opinion, but contributors should still be careful in describing the exact level of authority where relevant.

### Common Catholic Theology

This category refers to theological positions widely held across Catholic tradition and commonly taught in continuity with the faith, though not always proposed as a solemn definition in the strictest sense.

These claims may often be presented with strong confidence, but contributors should not confuse their widespread acceptance with formal dogmatic definition unless such definition exists.

### Accepted Theological Opinion

This category refers to theological views broadly accepted by many reliable Catholic theologians and often used in explanation or synthesis.

These views may be responsibly presented as strong theological judgments, but they should still be identified as theological interpretation rather than formally defined doctrine where appropriate.

### Permissible Theological Opinion

This category refers to views that may be held within Catholic bounds but are not required and may coexist with other legitimate Catholic positions.

These claims should be presented with explicit acknowledgment that legitimate theological diversity exists.

### Private Opinion

This category refers to the judgment of an individual theologian, writer, apologist, or contributor that does not carry ecclesial authority beyond its argumentative merit.

Private opinion should never be presented as Church teaching.

## Historical Classification

The following categories should be used when classifying historical claims.

### Direct Contemporary Evidence

This refers to evidence originating from the time or near the time of the event in question and directly bearing on the claim.

Examples may include direct documents, inscriptions, eyewitness testimony, or contemporaneous records.

### Primary Historical Evidence

This refers to early evidence closely related to the event, period, or figure under discussion, even if not fully direct in the strictest sense.

This category is strong historical material but may still require interpretation.

### Strong Historical Consensus

This refers to conclusions broadly accepted by competent historians based on substantial evidence.

Such claims may be stated with high historical confidence, while still remaining distinct from doctrinal authority.

### Moderate Historical Consensus

This refers to conclusions that are widely held but may involve more debate, less direct evidence, or stronger qualifications than claims in the strongest consensus category.

These claims should be expressed with appropriate modesty.

### Plausible Historical Reconstruction

This refers to a reasoned reconstruction supported by available evidence but not established with the same level of certainty as stronger consensus claims.

Such claims should be described as plausible rather than certain.

### Minority Historical View

This refers to a view held by some historians or scholars but not representing the dominant historical judgment.

If included, it should be identified as minority rather than presented as the standard conclusion.

### Historical Speculation

This refers to possibilities that lack sufficient evidence for confident historical assertion.

Historical speculation must never be presented as established fact.

## Biblical Interpretation

Biblical claims should be classified according to how directly the text supports them and what kind of interpretive process is involved.

### Explicit Biblical Teaching

This refers to what the text states directly and clearly in a way materially relevant to the claim.

### Strong Biblical Inference

This refers to conclusions drawn from the text with substantial support from context, canonical coherence, and established interpretation, even where the exact conclusion is not stated in one direct sentence.

### Typology

This refers to patterns of fulfillment, correspondence, and theological relation between persons, events, institutions, or covenants across salvation history.

Typology should be presented as typology rather than confused with direct literal assertion.

### Traditional Interpretation

This refers to interpretations long received and used within Catholic tradition, especially where Scripture is read in continuity with the Church’s theological life.

Such interpretations may carry significant theological weight even when distinct from purely grammatical analysis.

### Scholarly Interpretation

This refers to interpretations advanced through exegetical scholarship, literary analysis, historical study, or academic debate.

These should be presented according to the strength of their scholarly support and should not be confused automatically with binding teaching.

### Speculative Interpretation

This refers to interpretations that are possible but weakly supported, highly contested, or overly dependent on conjecture.

These should be labeled carefully and never presented as settled biblical meaning.

## Original Language Claims

Claims based on Greek, Hebrew, Latin, or Aramaic require special care because linguistic arguments are often overstated.

Contributors should distinguish among:

### Lexical Certainty

This refers to cases where the lexical meaning of a term is clear and broadly undisputed in the relevant context.

### Contextual Interpretation

This refers to cases where the meaning depends significantly on grammar, literary setting, usage, or theological context rather than on dictionary meaning alone.

These claims require more caution than simple lexical statements.

### Scholarly Debate

This refers to cases where scholars genuinely dispute how a term, phrase, construction, or idiom should be interpreted.

Where such debate exists, contributors should not present one interpretation as obvious unless the evidence genuinely justifies doing so.

## Archaeological Evidence

Archaeological claims should be classified according to what the evidence actually demonstrates.

### Direct Confirmation

This refers to archaeological evidence that directly supports a specific historical claim in a concrete way.

### Indirect Support

This refers to evidence that strengthens the plausibility of a claim without directly proving the exact point at issue.

### Contextual Evidence

This refers to evidence that helps explain the setting, culture, geography, or material world of the text or event.

### Disputed Interpretation

This refers to archaeological data whose meaning, identification, or significance remains contested.

Such evidence should not be presented with more certainty than the scholarly state of the question permits.

## Scholarly Claims

Modern scholarship should be classified according to the breadth and strength of support it possesses.

### Broad Scholarly Consensus

This refers to views accepted widely across competent scholarship with relatively little serious dispute.

### Majority Opinion

This refers to views held by many scholars but not without notable disagreement or qualification.

### Minority Opinion

This refers to views supported by a smaller portion of scholars and not representative of the dominant academic judgment.

### Emerging Hypothesis

This refers to proposals that are new, exploratory, or insufficiently tested to be treated as stable conclusions.

Contributors should not use scholarship selectively to create a false impression of consensus where there is only partial or minority support.

## Writing Rules

Contributors must never exaggerate certainty.

When evidence is limited, uncertainty should be acknowledged honestly. When a conclusion depends on inference, interpretation, probability, or scholarly reconstruction, that should be stated in a way that reflects the actual level of confidence warranted.

Whenever possible, contributors should explain why a conclusion is held rather than merely asserting it. This includes clarifying:

- what kind of evidence is being used
- what level of authority the claim carries
- whether the point is doctrinal, historical, interpretive, or speculative

Strong wording should be reserved for claims supported by strong authority or strong evidence.

## Practical Examples

The following examples illustrate how Catholic Veritas should classify different kinds of claims.

### Catholic Theology

Example:

The Trinity belongs to Divine Revelation and is taught as defined dogma.

Example:

A particular theological explanation of how grace and free will cooperate may belong to accepted theological opinion or permissible theological opinion depending on the claim being made.

### Church History

Example:

The existence of major ecumenical councils belongs to strong historical certainty grounded in primary historical evidence.

Example:

A detailed reconstruction of exactly how every participant at a council understood each intermediate stage of debate may belong to plausible historical reconstruction rather than direct historical certainty.

### Biblical Interpretation

Example:

The command to baptize belongs to explicit biblical teaching.

Example:

A typological connection between Old Testament figures and Marian theology may be classified as typology or traditional interpretation rather than explicit biblical teaching.

### Archaeology

Example:

An inscription naming a known ruler may count as direct confirmation for that ruler’s historical existence.

Example:

A proposed archaeological identification of a debated site may belong to disputed interpretation.

### Apologetics

Example:

The Catholic claim that Scripture and Tradition belong together may be presented as a theological conclusion grounded in Divine Revelation and authentic interpretation.

Example:

A modern apologetic argument about how a particular Protestant objection developed historically may belong to scholarly analysis or plausible historical reconstruction rather than defined doctrine.

## Editorial Checklist

Before publication, contributors should ask:

- Have I identified the correct level of authority behind this claim?
- Have I distinguished doctrine from theological opinion?
- Have I distinguished historical fact from historical probability?
- Have I distinguished explicit biblical teaching from inference or typology?
- Have I avoided presenting scholarly theories as settled certainty?
- Have I acknowledged real uncertainty where evidence is limited?
- Does the wording match the actual strength of the claim?
- Would a careful reader be able to tell what is certain, what is probable, and what is interpretive?

No article should be considered complete if its claims are more confident than its evidence or authority justify.
