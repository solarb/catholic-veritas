# Research Methodology

## Catholic Veritas

This document defines the official research methodology used throughout Catholic Veritas. It explains how every article should be researched before writing begins and establishes the repeatable research process that governs theology, Scripture, Church history, apologetics, biblical languages, and historical claims across the project.

This methodology applies equally to human contributors and AI contributors. Its purpose is to ensure that every article is built using the same research process, evidence standards, and verification methodology so that Catholic Veritas remains consistent, transparent, accurate, and trustworthy over the long term.

## Research Philosophy

Research in Catholic Veritas exists to discover and present truth rather than merely defend preconceived conclusions.

The goal is faithful Catholic apologetics grounded in evidence, not selective quotation, rhetorical convenience, or one-sided source collection. Contributors must not begin with a conclusion and then search only for supporting fragments. They must begin with the question, gather the relevant evidence honestly, and present the Catholic answer with fidelity, clarity, and intellectual integrity.

Because Catholic Veritas exists within the framework of the Catholic faith, research should be faithful to Divine Revelation and the authentic teaching of the Church. At the same time, that fidelity must never be used as an excuse for careless sourcing, weak historical reasoning, selective citation, or exaggerated certainty.

Truthfulness requires both doctrinal fidelity and research discipline.

## Research Workflow

Every article should be researched through a deliberate sequence of steps before publication.

### 1. Define the article objective

Begin by identifying what the article is supposed to do.

Examples include:

- explain a doctrine
- answer a question
- respond to an objection
- document a historical issue
- compare two positions

The objective determines what kinds of evidence will matter most and prevents the research process from becoming unfocused.

### 2. Identify the primary question

Every article should answer one primary question or explain one primary doctrine. That question should be defined before research begins.

Examples:

- What does the Catholic Church teach about Purgatory?
- Why do Catholics confess to a priest?
- What did the early Church believe about the Eucharist?

Defining the primary question helps contributors distinguish relevant evidence from tangential material.

### 3. Collect relevant Scripture

Gather all major biblical passages that directly or materially relate to the topic.

This includes:

- passages that support the Catholic position
- passages commonly raised in objection
- surrounding context needed for fair interpretation

Scripture collection should be broad enough to avoid proof-texting and disciplined enough to remain relevant to the article’s objective.

### 4. Study historical context

Research the historical, cultural, literary, and theological context required to understand the question accurately.

Depending on the topic, this may include:

- ancient Jewish context
- Greco-Roman background
- early Christian worship and belief
- later doctrinal development
- historical controversies

Context prevents misuse of isolated texts and helps clarify how claims should be interpreted.

### 5. Examine original languages where necessary

Consult Hebrew, Aramaic, Greek, or Latin only when language materially affects interpretation, theology, or apologetics.

Original-language research should clarify meaning, not serve as display. If language does not materially strengthen understanding, it should not dominate the research process.

### 6. Consult authoritative Church teaching

Gather the highest relevant Catholic authorities for the topic.

This may include:

- Catechism of the Catholic Church
- Ecumenical Councils
- papal definitions
- official Vatican documents
- magisterial teaching

This step establishes how the Church teaches the matter and prevents the article from drifting into private interpretation or theological confusion.

### 7. Examine Early Church Fathers

Research whether and how the Early Church Fathers, Apostolic Fathers, or Doctors of the Church addressed the subject.

Patristic evidence is especially important for:

- continuity of doctrine
- early interpretation of Scripture
- historical witness to Catholic belief and practice
- apologetics concerning the early Church

When patristic material is relevant, it should be examined directly where possible.

### 8. Review reliable historical evidence

Collect primary and secondary historical evidence relevant to the topic.

This may include:

- ancient historians
- letters
- inscriptions
- archaeology
- contemporary records
- reputable historical studies

Historical evidence should be evaluated according to the type of claim being made. Not all historical evidence serves the same purpose.

### 9. Review respected Catholic scholarship

Consult strong Catholic scholarly and apologetic treatments of the issue.

These sources help with:

- synthesis
- clarification
- identifying common misunderstandings
- tracing theological distinctions
- framing difficult objections responsibly

Catholic scholars help explain and defend the faith, but they should support higher authorities rather than replace them.

### 10. Identify common objections

For apologetics, doctrine, and controversial subjects, identify the strongest common objections before writing begins.

This includes:

- biblical objections
- historical objections
- doctrinal misunderstandings
- objections from Protestant, Orthodox, secular, Muslim, or skeptical perspectives where relevant

Objections should be researched fairly so that the eventual article responds to real arguments rather than weak caricatures.

### 11. Verify every major claim

Before writing, verify the article’s significant claims against the collected sources.

This includes checking:

- quotation accuracy
- historical claims
- doctrinal summaries
- language claims
- attribution
- chronology

No major claim should be included merely because it is familiar, persuasive, or widely repeated.

### 12. Write the article

Only after the research base is sufficiently gathered and verified should writing begin.

Writing should emerge from the evidence already assembled rather than forcing the research to justify a pre-written conclusion.

## Source Collection

Before writing begins, contributors should gather a balanced body of evidence appropriate to the topic.

Whenever relevant, source collection should include:

- biblical evidence
- historical evidence
- patristic evidence
- magisterial evidence
- scholarly evidence

Not every article will require each category equally, but contributors should think through each one before deciding what is relevant. This guards against one-dimensional research.

Source collection should be purposeful. The goal is not to gather the largest number of sources possible, but to gather the strongest and most relevant sources for the claim being made.

## Verification Standards

Every significant claim should be verified before publication.

Contributors must distinguish clearly between:

- direct evidence
- indirect evidence
- inference
- theological interpretation
- historical probability
- speculation

### Direct evidence

Evidence that explicitly supports the claim being made, such as a direct biblical text, conciliar statement, papal definition, or direct patristic quotation.

### Indirect evidence

Evidence that supports the broader framework or context but does not state the conclusion directly.

### Inference

A reasoned conclusion drawn from the available evidence. Inference may be legitimate, but it should not be presented as direct quotation or explicit proof.

### Theological interpretation

An explanation of revealed truth or doctrinal meaning. This must be distinguished from merely historical observation.

### Historical probability

A conclusion supported by historical evidence to a reasonable degree, but not established with absolute certainty.

### Speculation

A possibility that lacks sufficient evidence to be presented confidently. Speculation must never be disguised as established fact.

## Original Language Research

Hebrew, Greek, Latin, or Aramaic should be consulted when the original language genuinely clarifies the biblical text, doctrinal meaning, theological distinction, or apologetic issue under discussion.

Original-language research is especially useful when:

- a disputed term affects interpretation
- a translation issue is central to the argument
- lexical nuance materially strengthens understanding
- a theological distinction depends on wording

Contributors should avoid unnecessary technical discussion. Language analysis should illuminate meaning in plain English, not create the appearance of expertise for its own sake.

If language study does not materially improve the article, it should remain secondary.

## Historical Research

Historical claims must be researched with methodological care.

Contributors should distinguish among:

- primary historical sources
- secondary historical analysis
- scholarly consensus
- disputed historical theories

### Primary historical sources

These include documents, inscriptions, testimonies, records, and material evidence originating close to the period or event in question.

### Secondary historical analysis

These include later scholarly interpretations, reconstructions, and evaluations of the primary evidence.

### Scholarly consensus

This refers to conclusions that are broadly accepted by competent scholars, while still remaining open to refinement or qualification.

### Disputed historical theories

These are interpretations that remain debated, uncertain, or contested among scholars. They should be described honestly as disputed.

Historical research should never flatten these categories into one. Contributors must not present debated theories as settled fact or use isolated historical claims without checking context and credibility.

## Handling Objections

Every major apologetics article should identify the strongest opposing arguments before writing begins.

Opposing views must be represented fairly, clearly, and in terms that their strongest proponents would recognize as substantially accurate. Contributors should not respond to weak caricatures when stronger objections are well known and readily available.

When handling objections:

- identify the real claim being made
- distinguish biblical from historical objections
- distinguish theological disagreement from rhetorical criticism
- answer the strongest version of the objection when possible

Avoid strawman arguments. Catholic Veritas should be persuasive because it is truthful, fair, and rigorous, not because it chooses easy targets.

## Citation Workflow

The preferred order of citation should follow the established hierarchy of authority.

Contributors should normally prefer:

1. Scripture
2. Magisterial sources
3. Church Fathers
4. Historical sources
5. Catholic scholars
6. Academic scholarship

This order helps ensure that articles are built on the strongest and most authoritative foundation available.

Citation should move from primary and authoritative sources toward explanatory and secondary sources. Later scholarship should clarify and support earlier sources rather than replace them.

## Quality Assurance

Before publication, contributors should verify:

- theological accuracy
- historical accuracy
- biblical accuracy
- citation accuracy
- consistency with Catholic teaching
- consistency with existing Catholic Veritas articles

Quality assurance should include checking:

- whether the article answers its stated question
- whether quotations are accurate
- whether claims are properly sourced
- whether doctrinal distinctions are preserved
- whether the tone remains fair and disciplined
- whether the article duplicates existing content unnecessarily

An article is not ready merely because it is written. It is ready only when its claims, structure, and sources have been sufficiently verified.

## Long-Term Research Philosophy

Catholic Veritas values truth, transparency, intellectual honesty, and rigorous scholarship above speed or volume of publication.

The project seeks to become a trusted long-term Catholic apologetics knowledge base by applying the same disciplined research process to every article, whether the subject is theology, Scripture, history, language, or apologetics.

Over time, trust will depend not only on what Catholic Veritas says, but on how carefully it researches, verifies, and presents what it says. For that reason, research methodology is not an optional background task. It is a permanent part of the integrity of the project.
