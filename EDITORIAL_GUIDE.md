# Editorial Guide

## Catholic Veritas

This document defines the permanent editorial standards of Catholic Veritas. Every contributor, whether human or AI, must follow this guide whenever writing, revising, or expanding any article.

Its purpose is to ensure that every page across the project appears as though it was written by the same author, regardless of when it was created, who created it, or what platform hosts the content in the future. This guide establishes the long-term standards for writing philosophy, editorial method, formatting, citation, tone, language, terminology, and consistency.

## Editorial Philosophy

Catholic Veritas should be:

- accurate
- charitable
- evidence-based
- timeless
- readable
- well organised

Every article should educate before attempting to persuade. The goal is not merely to win arguments, but to help readers understand Catholic truth with clarity and fairness.

Truth must always take precedence over rhetoric. Contributors must never sacrifice accuracy, nuance, or intellectual honesty in order to make an argument sound stronger, simpler, or more emotionally forceful.

Articles should be written for long-term usefulness. The project should avoid reactive writing, trend-driven framing, or short-lived language that will age badly. Content should remain understandable, relevant, and trustworthy years after publication.

## Tone

The writing tone of Catholic Veritas should be:

- respectful
- scholarly
- calm
- confident
- objective
- charitable

The site may disagree clearly and firmly with ideas, arguments, or interpretations, but it must never attack persons or groups unfairly.

Contributors must avoid:

- sarcasm
- mockery
- emotional language
- sensationalism
- unnecessary exaggeration

Disagreement should be principled, evidence-based, and measured. The tone should communicate seriousness and credibility rather than hostility or defensiveness.

## Reading Level

Catholic Veritas should be written for educated general readers.

Articles should be accessible to beginners without becoming shallow or simplistic. Advanced readers should still find them useful, precise, and well-structured.

Contributors should avoid unnecessary academic jargon. When technical theological, philosophical, historical, or linguistic terms are necessary, those terms should be explained clearly in plain English.

The aim is not to simplify difficult subjects into vagueness, but to make them understandable without needless complexity.

## Article Structure

Every article should follow a consistent and recognizable structure. Not every article requires every possible section, but the organizational logic should remain stable across the site.

Where appropriate, articles should include:

- Summary
- Biblical Evidence
- Historical Context
- Original Languages
- Early Church Fathers
- Catholic Teaching
- Common Objections
- References
- Related Articles

### Structural principles

- Begin by making the page’s main subject clear.
- Organize material from foundational explanation to supporting evidence.
- Distinguish clearly between explanation, evidence, interpretation, and response to objections.
- Use headings to guide the reader naturally through the article.
- Keep sections proportionate to their importance.

The structure should support clarity and retrieval. Readers should be able to scan an article, understand its scope, and locate key material without confusion.

## Scripture Standards

Scripture should be treated with precision, reverence, and contextual accuracy.

Contributors should:

- quote Scripture accurately
- preserve literary and theological context
- distinguish direct quotations from commentary
- cite chapter and verse consistently
- avoid isolated proof-texting that distorts meaning

Scripture should be the primary source whenever applicable, especially when discussing doctrines, biblical objections, or questions of revelation.

### Scripture handling rules

- Quotations should match a chosen text accurately.
- Commentary should not be blended into quotations in a way that confuses the source.
- When a passage is disputed, the article should explain the relevant context rather than assuming the meaning is obvious.
- When comparing interpretations, the Catholic reading should be explained fairly and clearly.

Scripture should illuminate the argument, not merely decorate it.

## Original Languages

Greek, Hebrew, Aramaic, or Latin should be used only when they genuinely clarify a biblical or theological point.

Contributors must not include original-language discussion merely to impress readers or create a false appearance of authority.

When original-language material is used:

- explain the term clearly in plain English
- show why it matters to the topic
- avoid unnecessary technical detail
- distinguish lexical meaning from theological interpretation

Original-language discussion should serve understanding, not display expertise for its own sake.

## Historical Standards

Historical writing in Catholic Veritas must distinguish clearly between:

- historical fact
- historical consensus
- historical debate
- theological interpretation

Contributors must never present speculation as established history.

Historical claims should be careful, proportionate, and evidence-based. Where evidence is limited or scholarly conclusions remain debated, that uncertainty should be stated clearly rather than hidden behind overconfident language.

Historical arguments should not be simplified merely because a simpler version is easier to use in apologetics.

## Church Fathers

Quotations from the Early Church Fathers should be used carefully and consistently.

Contributors should prefer direct quotations where practical and should always identify:

- author
- work
- approximate date

The Fathers should be used to illustrate continuity, witness, development, and historical context. They should not be used as replacements for Scripture or as isolated quotations detached from context.

### Patristic usage rules

- Use Fathers when they genuinely clarify the continuity of Catholic teaching.
- Avoid quoting patristic sources in a misleading or decontextualized way.
- Do not imply unanimous patristic agreement where there was legitimate diversity of expression.
- When a Father is used in argument, explain why the citation matters.

## Catholic Sources

Catholic Veritas should prefer primary Catholic sources whenever possible.

Preferred sources include:

- Catechism of the Catholic Church
- Ecumenical Councils
- Papal documents
- Vatican documents

These sources should anchor doctrinal explanation whenever relevant.

Contributors must clearly distinguish between:

- defined doctrine
- authoritative teaching
- theological opinion
- devotional expression
- disciplinary or historical practice

No secondary summary should be allowed to replace a primary Catholic source when the primary source is reasonably available and relevant.

## Citations

Catholic Veritas must use a consistent citation style across the site. The specific format may be simple, but it must remain uniform from article to article.

Consistency is more important than adopting any particular academic citation system.

### Citation principles

- Cite major claims consistently.
- Prefer primary sources when possible.
- Use citations that are clear enough for readers to trace the source.
- Keep format stable across the site.
- Do not mix citation styles casually within the same article.

### Scripture citations

Scripture should be cited consistently by book, chapter, and verse.

Example pattern:

- John 6:51-58
- Matthew 16:18-19

### Catechism citations

Cite the Catechism consistently by section number.

Example pattern:

- Catechism of the Catholic Church, 846

### Church Father citations

Identify:

- author
- work
- approximate date

Example pattern:

- Ignatius of Antioch, *Letter to the Smyrnaeans*, c. AD 107

### Council citations

Identify the council and, where relevant, the decree, session, or canon clearly and consistently.

Example pattern:

- Council of Trent, Session 6

### Historical works

Identify the author and work clearly enough for readers to distinguish the source and its historical role.

The important rule is consistency, clarity, and traceability.

## Formatting Standards

All pages should share the same visual rhythm. Formatting exists to support readability, scanning, and consistency across the site.

### Heading hierarchy

- Use headings consistently from broad sections to narrower subsections.
- Avoid skipping levels unnecessarily.
- Headings should describe content clearly, not merely decorate the page.

### Bullet lists

- Use bullet lists for parallel ideas, grouped examples, or concise reference points.
- Keep bullet phrasing consistent within a list.
- Do not overuse long bullet sequences where prose would be clearer.

### Numbered lists

- Use numbered lists when sequence, priority, or process matters.
- Keep numbering meaningful rather than ornamental.

### Block quotations

- Use block quotations for important primary-source excerpts.
- Keep quotations relevant and proportionate.
- Introduce and explain quotations rather than dropping them into the page without context.

### Tables

- Use tables only when they improve clarity through comparison, summary, or categorization.
- Keep tables simple and easy to scan.
- Do not use tables merely for decoration.

### Timelines

- Use timelines when chronology matters to understanding.
- Keep them concise, factual, and clearly ordered.

### Callout boxes

- Use callout boxes sparingly for important clarifications, definitions, warnings about common misunderstandings, or key distinctions.
- Do not overload pages with visual emphasis.

### Emphasis

- Use emphasis sparingly and consistently.
- Prefer emphasis for genuine importance, not rhetorical force.
- Avoid excessive bolding, italics, or other visual stress.

Formatting should support a calm and disciplined reading experience.

## Images and Diagrams

Images should clarify content rather than decorate it.

Diagrams should be:

- accurate
- simple
- consistent
- educational

Contributors should prefer images and diagrams that genuinely help readers understand chronology, structure, relationships, geography, or theological distinctions.

Because the current platform is WordPress.com Free, contributors should prefer static images and straightforward visual elements that work reliably within native platform constraints.

No editorial recommendation should assume unavailable platform features as the default approach.

## Internal Links

Every article should naturally link to related topics where such links genuinely help the reader continue learning.

Internal links should:

- guide readers to prerequisite background
- connect related doctrines or historical issues
- support deeper exploration
- reinforce the site’s overall knowledge structure

Contributors should avoid excessive linking. Links should help the reader without interrupting the flow or turning ordinary prose into a dense field of distractions.

Each article should be written with the awareness that it belongs to a larger reference system, not as an isolated page.

## Revision Standards

When revising existing articles, contributors should:

- preserve accurate content whenever possible
- improve clarity
- improve organisation
- remove duplication
- maintain consistency with the rest of the site

Contributors must not rewrite articles merely for stylistic preference. Revision should serve a real improvement in accuracy, readability, organization, sourcing, or consistency.

When substantial changes are made, the revised article should still feel like part of the same site rather than the product of a competing editorial philosophy.

## Editorial Checklist

Before publishing or finalizing any article, contributors should confirm:

- Is the article accurate?
- Is every major claim supported?
- Is the structure consistent with the rest of the site?
- Is the tone charitable and respectful?
- Are Scripture quotations accurate and properly contextualized?
- Are doctrinal claims clearly distinguished from opinion or speculation?
- Are historical claims presented honestly and proportionately?
- Are related articles linked where appropriate?
- Does the article remain useful as a standalone reference?
- Is the formatting consistent and readable?
- Does the article contribute something distinct rather than duplicate another page?
- Would this still be valuable ten years from now?
