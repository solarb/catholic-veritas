# Project Constitution

## Catholic Veritas

This document is the governing constitution of Catholic Veritas. It exists to preserve the identity, mission, standards, and long-term direction of the project across many years of growth and many different contributors. It is the highest internal authority for the project, second only to the official teachings of the Catholic Church.

Every future decision regarding content, architecture, writing, design, and development must conform to this constitution. Its purpose is not to accelerate change, but to prevent drift, preserve clarity, and ensure that Catholic Veritas remains faithful to its mission over time.

## Preamble

Catholic Veritas exists because the Catholic faith deserves to be explained, defended, and preserved with fidelity, seriousness, and intellectual honesty. It exists to serve readers who are seeking truth, understanding, and clear access to Catholic teaching through a durable and trustworthy body of work.

This project is not meant to be reinvented by each new contributor. Every future contributor inherits a responsibility not to redefine the mission, but to preserve and strengthen it. Human contributors and AI assistants alike are custodians of an existing purpose, not authors of a new one.

The task of every contributor is therefore one of stewardship. The mission, standards, and philosophy of Catholic Veritas must be protected even as the project grows in size, depth, and usefulness.

## Core Mission

The mission of Catholic Veritas is to explain, defend, and preserve the Catholic faith using Scripture, Sacred Tradition, history, reason, and the continuous teaching authority of the Catholic Church.

The project exists to make Catholic truth more understandable, more accessible, and more durable for readers across different backgrounds and levels of knowledge. It aims to provide clear and faithful explanations that remain useful for many years rather than merely addressing short-term questions or trends.

## Core Philosophy

Catholic Veritas is:

- a Catholic apologetics knowledge base
- a theological reference library
- an educational resource
- a research companion

Catholic Veritas is not:

- a personal blog
- a devotional website
- a theology textbook
- an RCIA course
- a discussion forum
- an opinion website

This distinction matters. The project should not drift into casual self-expression, reactive publishing, informal commentary, or undefined content accumulation. Its purpose is to become a reliable, long-term reference resource grounded in fidelity, clarity, and disciplined organization.

## Non-Negotiable Principles

The following principles are permanent and must not be set aside for convenience, popularity, speed, or stylistic preference:

- Fidelity to the Catholic Church.
- Scripture interpreted within Sacred Tradition.
- Historical honesty.
- Intellectual integrity.
- Charity toward those who disagree.
- Truth before popularity.
- Accuracy before persuasion.
- Clarity before complexity.
- Long-term maintainability before short-term novelty.

No contributor may knowingly weaken these principles. If a future proposal conflicts with them, the proposal must be rejected or revised.

## Authority Hierarchy

Catholic Veritas must operate according to a clear order of authority.

1. Sacred Scripture
2. Sacred Tradition
3. The Magisterium
4. Ecumenical Councils
5. Catechism of the Catholic Church
6. Official Vatican documents
7. Church Fathers
8. Reliable historical scholarship

This hierarchy governs how truth claims are weighed and how explanations are framed. Lower authorities may help explain higher ones, but they must never be used to overturn them. Personal opinion, preference, or rhetoric must never override authoritative sources.

When multiple sources are used together, contributors must preserve their proper relationship rather than flattening them into a single undifferentiated level of authority.

## Content Principles

Every article in Catholic Veritas should:

- answer one primary question or explain one primary doctrine
- remain useful as a standalone reference
- be understandable without requiring readers to complete previous articles
- avoid unnecessary duplication
- link to related articles where appropriate

Each page should have a clear scope, a stable purpose, and a place within the broader knowledge base. Articles should contribute to the coherence of the whole rather than existing as isolated or repetitive pieces of writing.

## Writing Principles

Every article should be:

- accurate
- charitable
- evidence-based
- logically organised
- well sourced
- timeless
- understandable

Contributors must avoid sensationalism, unnecessary speculation, and polemics. Writing should be calm, disciplined, and clear. The purpose is not to impress, provoke, or overwhelm, but to explain faithfully and helpfully.

Whenever possible, articles should be written in a way that remains useful beyond the immediate moment in which they are created. Durable clarity should be preferred over trend-driven phrasing or reactive framing.

## Historical Standards

Contributors must clearly distinguish between:

- established historical fact
- theological doctrine
- scholarly consensus
- historical debate
- informed speculation

These categories must not be blurred for rhetorical advantage. Historical uncertainty must be acknowledged honestly, and speculation must never be presented as fact. Contributors must not simplify history in ways that materially distort evidence, chronology, context, or scholarly complexity.

The apologetic usefulness of a claim does not make it historically true. Historical standards must remain rigorous even when a more convenient narrative would be easier to present.

## Doctrinal Standards

Catholic teaching must always be presented faithfully.

When discussing disputed questions, contributors must clearly distinguish between:

- defined doctrine
- authoritative teaching
- theological opinion
- historical practice

No contributor may present theological speculation as binding doctrine, nor treat disciplinary or historical variation as proof of doctrinal instability. Distinctions that matter in Catholic theology must be preserved with precision.

If uncertainty exists about whether a claim accurately reflects Catholic teaching, that claim must be reviewed and clarified before it is treated as settled presentation.

## Decision Principles

Before recommending any new page, category, or structural change, contributors must ask:

1. Does this improve the knowledge base?
2. Does this duplicate existing material?
3. Can this become a section instead?
4. Will this remain useful ten years from now?
5. Does it strengthen the site's long-term architecture?

These questions exist to prevent fragmentation, redundancy, and short-sighted decisions. Expansion should be deliberate. The existence of a possible topic is not, by itself, a sufficient reason to create a new page or structural layer.

## Preservation of Consistency

Future contributors should preserve:

- terminology
- formatting
- article structure
- citation style
- navigation philosophy
- internal linking philosophy

Consistency should be preferred over novelty. Readers should encounter a unified knowledge base rather than a patchwork of styles, structures, and editorial assumptions. Any change that improves one page while weakening overall consistency should be treated with caution.

Consistency is not cosmetic. It is structural and intellectual. It supports trust, readability, and long-term maintainability across the entire project.

## Future Development

Catholic Veritas may grow to thousands of articles over time. Such growth must occur by strengthening the existing architecture rather than continually inventing new structures.

Expansion should deepen and refine the knowledge base without abandoning its governing philosophy. New content, new categories, and new systems should be introduced only when they support clarity, coherence, and long-term usability.

The overall philosophy of the project should remain stable even as its scale increases. Growth must never become an excuse for disorder, inconsistency, or loss of identity.

## Constitutional Amendment

This constitution should only be changed deliberately by the project owner.

Routine contributors, including AI assistants, must not rewrite the philosophy, standards, mission, or identity of Catholic Veritas without explicit approval. Suggestions may be made, but the constitution itself is not to be casually revised in the course of ordinary contribution.

Any amendment to this document should be treated as a serious governance decision because it affects the long-term integrity and continuity of the entire project.
