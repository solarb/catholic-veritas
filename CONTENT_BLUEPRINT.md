# Content Blueprint

## Catholic Veritas

This document defines the content philosophy of Catholic Veritas. It explains what every article should accomplish, how articles should function conceptually within the site, and how the entire knowledge base should grow over time.

This is not a writing guide and it is not a page template. It is a blueprint describing how content should function within the Catholic Veritas knowledge base.

Every contributor should understand this document before creating any new article. Its purpose is to ensure that every page contributes to a coherent, searchable, evidence-based Catholic apologetics knowledge base rather than becoming an isolated piece of writing.

## Content Philosophy

Catholic Veritas is a searchable apologetics database and theological reference library.

Every article exists to answer one important question or explain one important doctrine. Articles should not exist merely to fill space, chase trends, or duplicate what has already been said elsewhere in the knowledge base.

Each article should remain independently useful. A reader should be able to arrive on a page directly, understand its purpose, and receive a meaningful answer without needing to read several earlier articles first.

The website should function as a reference library rather than a sequential textbook. It should support direct lookup, long-form exploration, apologetics research, theological clarification, and structured study without depending on fixed reading order.

## Content Objectives

Every article should satisfy one primary objective. That objective should be clear from the beginning and should govern the structure, evidence, and scope of the page.

Common primary objectives include:

- Explain
- Defend
- Clarify
- Compare
- Refute
- Document
- Teach

An article may contain secondary effects, but it should not attempt to accomplish multiple unrelated objectives at once.

For example:

- a page explaining a doctrine should not become a broad history page unless history is necessary to the explanation
- a page refuting an objection should not become a full encyclopedia page on every related doctrine
- a page comparing traditions should not drift into a general devotional reflection

Focused content is clearer, more searchable, easier to maintain, and less likely to duplicate other pages.

## Types of Articles

Catholic Veritas should develop a stable range of article types. Each type serves a distinct role within the knowledge base.

### Doctrine

Purpose:

- explain a defined Catholic teaching or major theological concept

Doctrine articles should provide clear, faithful, and durable explanations of what the Church teaches and why it matters.

### Scripture

Purpose:

- explain biblical foundations, canon, interpretation, and difficult passages

Scripture articles should help readers understand how the Catholic Church reads and uses Scripture.

### Church History

Purpose:

- document key events, eras, developments, and controversies in the life of the Church

These articles should preserve historical accuracy and distinguish fact from interpretation.

### Church Fathers

Purpose:

- present the witness, writings, and relevance of the Early Church Fathers

These articles should demonstrate continuity, context, and historical witness without reducing the Fathers to isolated quotation collections.

### Biblical Studies

Purpose:

- address interpretive questions, literary context, thematic studies, and biblical reasoning

These pages support deeper scriptural understanding beyond basic proof-texting.

### Apologetics

Purpose:

- answer objections, misunderstandings, criticisms, and common disputes related to Catholic teaching

Apologetics articles should be evidence-based, charitable, and tightly scoped.

### Comparative Religion

Purpose:

- compare Catholicism with other religious or theological systems fairly and clearly

These pages should explain both similarities and differences without caricature.

### Moral Theology

Purpose:

- explain Catholic teaching on human conduct, conscience, virtue, sin, and the moral life

### Sacraments

Purpose:

- explain sacramental theology, biblical foundations, and the role of each sacrament in Catholic life

### Biography

Purpose:

- present key figures whose lives or thought matter for Catholic theology, history, apologetics, or spirituality

Biography pages should remain relevant to the knowledge base rather than becoming generic life summaries.

### Councils

Purpose:

- document major ecumenical councils, their teachings, historical context, and significance

### Papal Documents

Purpose:

- explain important papal texts, their authority, context, and theological importance

### Biblical Languages

Purpose:

- clarify important Hebrew, Aramaic, Greek, or Latin terms where language materially affects interpretation

### Frequently Asked Questions

Purpose:

- answer direct, high-frequency reader questions in a concise and search-friendly form

These pages should often point to fuller doctrine or apologetics pages rather than trying to replace them completely.

## Article Scope

Every article should answer one primary question.

That question may be stated explicitly or function as the article’s controlling purpose. If additional topics become too large, they should become related articles rather than causing the original page to expand indefinitely.

Each article should therefore have:

- one primary scope
- one central purpose
- one identifiable place in the knowledge base

A page may refer to several surrounding themes, but it must remain disciplined. If a contributor cannot state clearly what the page is primarily trying to accomplish, the page scope is too vague.

## Knowledge Relationships

Catholic Veritas should grow as an interconnected system of complementary pages.

Articles should support one another through internal links rather than repeating the same explanations in slightly different wording. The aim is not maximum page count, but maximum clarity and usefulness.

Healthy knowledge relationships include:

- doctrine pages supported by Scripture pages
- apologetics pages supported by doctrinal pages
- history pages supported by council or biography pages
- question pages supported by deeper explanatory pages
- language pages supporting Scripture or doctrine pages when needed

Pages should complement one another, not compete with one another.

## Progressive Depth

Catholic Veritas should support multiple levels of readership within the same knowledge system.

Broadly speaking, content should be useful to:

- Beginner
- Intermediate
- Advanced

This does not mean every article must contain every level equally. It means the knowledge base as a whole should allow readers to move from accessible explanation into deeper material without confusion.

Where possible, individual pages should also support layered reading:

- a beginner should be able to understand the central answer
- an intermediate reader should be able to explore argument and context
- an advanced reader should be able to follow evidence, distinctions, and cross-references more deeply

Readers should be able to stop at any level while still receiving a complete and truthful answer.

## Evidence Hierarchy

Arguments in Catholic Veritas should normally be presented in a principled order of evidence and explanation.

Typical order:

1. Scripture
2. Context
3. Original Language (when relevant)
4. Historical Evidence
5. Church Fathers
6. Catholic Teaching
7. Common Objections
8. Conclusion

Not every article requires every element, and the order should always serve the article’s primary objective. The sequence is a guiding logic, not a rigid formula.

For example:

- a historical article may foreground chronology and evidence before doctrinal discussion
- a doctrinal article may foreground Catholic teaching while still grounding itself in Scripture
- a language-focused article may bring original-language analysis closer to the beginning

The principle is that arguments should move from source and context toward explanation, clarification, and response.

## Cross-Link Philosophy

Internal links should expand understanding rather than replace explanation.

Articles should link to related pages when those pages genuinely deepen the reader’s understanding, provide needed background, or offer a more focused treatment of a connected subject.

Cross-links should:

- support movement through the knowledge base
- reduce unnecessary repetition
- help readers discover broader and narrower topics
- strengthen the coherence of the overall reference system

Cross-links should not be used as a substitute for answering the page’s own primary question. Every article must still stand on its own.

## Future Growth

The knowledge base should grow by increasing depth within existing categories before expanding into new categories.

Growth should occur through:

- deeper treatment of established topics
- better doctrinal coverage
- stronger question coverage
- more precise historical and patristic support
- improved cross-linking between existing topics

Architectural stability should be maintained across time. New categories should be introduced rarely and only when existing categories can no longer absorb new material clearly.

The site should grow like a library becoming richer and better organized, not like a collection of disconnected posts multiplying without discipline.

## Long-Term Vision

Catholic Veritas should eventually become a comprehensive Catholic reference library that is comparable in usefulness to major apologetics and theological resources while remaining faithful to its own identity, mission, architecture, and editorial philosophy.

Its long-term value should come not only from the quantity of its content, but from the coherence, trustworthiness, depth, clarity, and durability of the knowledge base as a whole.

The project should mature into a resource that readers can consult repeatedly for explanation, study, apologetics, research, and long-term theological understanding without losing the disciplined identity that makes Catholic Veritas distinct.
