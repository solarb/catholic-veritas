# Site Architecture

## Catholic Veritas

This document defines the permanent information architecture of Catholic Veritas. It exists to preserve structural clarity, navigational coherence, and long-term scalability as the website grows into a large Catholic apologetics and theological knowledge base.

Its purpose is to ensure that every future contributor, whether human or AI, expands the site consistently without creating duplicate content, unnecessary categories, or structural drift. This document should remain stable over many years and should guide all architectural decisions during the Foundation stage and beyond.

## Project Reference

- **Project:** Catholic Veritas
- **Website:** [https://catholicveritas73.wordpress.com/](https://catholicveritas73.wordpress.com/)
- **Platform:** WordPress.com (Free Plan)
- **Current Stage:** Foundation
- **Future Goal:** Scale into one of the world's largest Catholic apologetics and theological knowledge bases

## Design Philosophy

Catholic Veritas is a knowledge base, not a sequential course. Readers should be able to begin with any article and still find enough context to understand the subject, discover related topics, and continue learning naturally.

The site should not assume that readers arrive in a fixed order. Some will begin with foundational questions, some with a doctrinal controversy, some with a historical issue, and some with a highly specific apologetics question. The architecture must therefore support non-linear reading while still preserving a clear overall structure.

Navigation should help readers discover related topics naturally. Every important page should connect readers both upward into broader themes and outward into related topics. The architecture should prioritize:

- discoverability
- clarity
- searchability
- doctrinal coherence
- long-term scalability
- structural stability

The site should grow as an interconnected reference library rather than as a collection of isolated posts.

## Parent and Child Pages

Not every topic deserves equal structural depth. Contributors must use parent pages and child pages deliberately.

### A page should become a parent page when:

- it covers a broad subject with multiple distinct subtopics
- readers naturally search for both the broad topic and its narrower parts
- the page would become too long or unfocused if all subtopics remained inside it
- the subject functions as a navigational hub across multiple related pages
- future growth is likely and predictable

### Information should remain a child page when:

- it is a distinct subtopic within a broader theme
- it can be explained clearly in relation to a parent topic
- readers benefit from a stable hierarchy
- the page answers a more specific question than the parent page

### Information should remain a section within an existing page when:

- it is too narrow to justify its own URL
- it is not likely to attract direct search interest
- it cannot stand well on its own
- separating it would create thin or duplicative content
- the topic is best understood within immediate context

Parent pages should act as hubs, not as content dumps. Child pages should carry the detail.

## Creating New Pages

Before creating a new page, contributors must determine:

- Does this deserve its own URL?
- Can this become a section of an existing article?
- Does it duplicate another article?
- Will people naturally search for this topic?

The default should be to expand existing articles before creating new pages.

### Create a new page when:

- the topic has a clear, independent purpose
- the topic answers a distinct primary question
- the topic has lasting search value
- the topic can remain useful as a standalone reference
- separating it improves clarity and long-term structure

### Do not create a new page when:

- the material is only a small subsection of a broader subject
- the content substantially overlaps an existing article
- the new page would be thin, repetitive, or dependent on another page for basic clarity
- the primary motivation is novelty rather than structural usefulness

## Internal Linking Strategy

Catholic Veritas should develop a dense and intentional web of internal links. Internal linking is not optional decoration; it is part of the site’s architecture.

Every article should contain, where appropriate:

- prerequisite articles
- related articles
- next recommended reading
- broader topic links

### Prerequisite articles

These help readers understand foundational ideas before tackling a more specific topic.

Examples:

- a page on `Purgatory` should point to broader pages on `Salvation`, `Judgment`, and `The Communion of Saints`
- a page on `Sola Scriptura` should point to `Sacred Tradition` and `The Magisterium`

### Related articles

These connect conceptually adjacent topics.

Examples:

- `The Papacy` should link to `Apostolic Succession`, `Authority in the Church`, and `The Magisterium`
- `Immaculate Conception` should link to `Mary`, `Original Sin`, and `Mother of God`

### Next recommended reading

These guide readers into logical follow-up articles.

Examples:

- from a beginner page, move to a deeper doctrine page
- from a question page, move to a full doctrinal explanation
- from an objection page, move to a doctrinal and historical treatment

### Broader topic links

These orient the reader upward into the hierarchy.

Examples:

- a child page should link back to its parent topic
- a narrow apologetics page should link back to its doctrinal home

Cross-references should be generous but disciplined. Links should clarify, not clutter.

## Navigation Philosophy

Navigation should support:

- beginners
- researchers
- RCIA students
- debate preparation
- quick reference

Readers should never feel lost. Every important page should make clear:

- what the page is about
- what broader topic it belongs to
- what related topics should be read next
- where to go for foundational background

Navigation should not depend entirely on menus. It should also be reinforced through page structure, parent-child relationships, related reading, and contextual links within articles.

The site should reward both direct search entry and long-form exploration.

## Naming Conventions

Article titles should be clear, concise, descriptive, and search-friendly.

Contributors should prefer titles that people naturally search for. Titles should help both human readers and future site organization.

### Naming rules

- Use concise, descriptive names.
- Prefer common and recognizable phrasing over unnecessarily academic wording.
- Make the main topic obvious from the title.
- Use question titles when readers naturally search in question form.
- Use doctrine titles when the article is primarily explanatory rather than interrogative.
- Keep sibling titles parallel where possible.

### Prefer titles such as:

- Why Do Catholics Pray to Saints?
- What Is the Papacy?
- The Immaculate Conception
- History of the Biblical Canon

### Avoid:

- vague titles
- clever titles that hide the topic
- excessively academic phrasing when a simpler title is clearer
- multiple pages with slightly different wording but the same scope

## Categories vs Articles

Contributors must clearly distinguish between different kinds of pages.

### Category Pages

Purpose:

- organize broad domains
- introduce major themes
- guide readers into child pages

Category pages are navigational and orienting by nature.

### Reference Pages

Purpose:

- provide structured lookup material
- support readers needing fast orientation or summary

Examples:

- glossary pages
- timelines
- index-style resource pages

### Doctrine Pages

Purpose:

- explain a defined Catholic teaching or major theological concept

These should usually be stable, evergreen, and central to the knowledge base.

### Historical Pages

Purpose:

- explain events, developments, controversies, or historical evidence

These should distinguish clearly between fact, interpretation, and debate.

### Question Pages

Purpose:

- answer natural reader questions directly
- serve search-first entry points

These should often link into doctrine pages rather than duplicating them entirely.

### Resource Pages

Purpose:

- gather reading paths, study tools, cross-topic indexes, or structured guidance

These support navigation and learning rather than replacing core content.

## Avoiding Duplication

Duplication weakens clarity, authority, and maintainability. Catholic Veritas should not allow multiple pages that answer the same primary question in substantially the same way.

If two pages substantially overlap, contributors must:

- merge them
- restructure them
- or redefine their scope

### Rules for avoiding duplication

- One primary question per page.
- One primary doctrinal explanation per topic.
- One canonical home for each major subject.
- Question pages should point to doctrine pages rather than recreate them fully.
- Objection pages should critique or clarify from a distinct angle, not duplicate full doctrinal exposition unless necessary.

Before creating or expanding a page, contributors should check whether an existing article already covers the same conceptual ground.

## Long-Term Growth

Catholic Veritas should be designed to support several thousand articles without losing clarity.

Growth should occur by strengthening the existing structure rather than creating new top-level categories unnecessarily. In most cases, expansion should happen by:

- deepening existing parent pages
- creating child pages under stable domains
- strengthening cross-references
- building better reference and index pages
- refining scope boundaries between related articles

The site should aim for stability over constant reorganisation. Frequent structural reinvention creates confusion, weakens internal consistency, and increases maintenance cost.

As the site grows, new complexity should be absorbed through better hierarchy and clearer scope, not through category proliferation.

## Architectural Principles

- One primary purpose per page.
- Clear hierarchy.
- Strong internal linking.
- Minimal duplication.
- Easy navigation.
- Long-term maintainability.
- Search-first organisation.
- Consistent taxonomy.
- Stable top-level categories.
- Expand existing structures before inventing new ones.
