# Source Authority

## Catholic Veritas

This document defines the authority hierarchy of every source used throughout Catholic Veritas. It establishes how contributors, whether human or AI, should evaluate, prioritize, reconcile, and cite sources when researching or writing content.

It is intended to serve as the permanent research authority framework for the project. Its purpose is to ensure that every article is grounded in the strongest and most authoritative evidence available and that source usage remains disciplined, transparent, and faithful to the mission of Catholic Veritas.

## Introduction

Establishing a clear hierarchy of authority is essential for a Catholic apologetics knowledge base because not every source carries the same weight. Some sources are authoritative because of their relationship to Divine Revelation and the teaching authority of the Catholic Church. Others are valuable because they provide historical witness, theological explanation, linguistic clarification, or scholarly context.

The authority of a source depends upon its relationship to Divine Revelation and to the authentic teaching authority of the Church. A source may be useful without being authoritative in the highest sense. It may be illuminating without being binding. It may be historically important without serving as a doctrinal standard.

For Catholic Veritas to remain trustworthy, contributors must know which sources stand highest, which sources help interpret them, which sources offer historical corroboration, and which sources function mainly as supporting tools.

## Source Authority Hierarchy

### Level 1 — Divine Revelation

This is the highest level of authority.

It includes:

- Sacred Scripture
- Sacred Tradition

Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition together form the one Deposit of Faith as taught by *Dei Verbum*. They must never be treated as competing authorities or separated from one another in a way that distorts Catholic teaching. Scripture is not interpreted in isolation from Tradition, and Tradition is not understood apart from its relationship to the revealed Word of God.

No lower level of authority may override Divine Revelation.

### Level 2 — Authentic Interpretation

This level includes the authoritative interpretation of the Deposit of Faith.

It includes:

- The Magisterium
- Ecumenical Councils
- Papal Definitions
- Ordinary and Universal Magisterium
- Catechism of the Catholic Church
- Official Vatican Documents

These sources authentically interpret the Deposit of Faith but do not add new public revelation. Their role is to clarify, safeguard, teach, and transmit what has been revealed in Scripture and Tradition.

When Catholic Veritas explains doctrine, these sources carry decisive weight. Contributors must distinguish among degrees of authority where appropriate, but the overall principle remains that authentic Catholic teaching governs doctrinal presentation.

### Level 3 — Apostolic Witness

This level includes early witnesses to the apostolic faith and its transmission.

It includes:

- Early Church Fathers
- Apostolic Fathers
- Doctors of the Church where appropriate

These sources provide historical witness to how the apostolic faith was understood, preserved, taught, defended, and handed on. They are especially important for demonstrating continuity, early interpretation, liturgical life, and the development of doctrinal expression.

They do not stand above Divine Revelation or the authentic teaching authority of the Church, but they are of great importance for apologetics, historical theology, and the study of Catholic continuity.

### Level 4 — Historical Witness

This level includes historical sources that provide context, corroboration, or evidence relevant to the biblical world, the early Church, or Catholic claims in history.

Examples include:

- Josephus
- Eusebius
- ancient inscriptions
- archaeological discoveries
- contemporary historical records

These sources are valuable because they help establish chronology, setting, historical plausibility, cultural context, and external witness. They can corroborate or illuminate aspects of biblical and Church history, but they do not determine doctrine.

Their authority is historical, not magisterial.

### Level 5 — Catholic Scholarship

This level includes respected Catholic theologians, apologists, historians, and scholars who explain, defend, synthesize, and clarify the faith.

Examples include:

- St. Thomas Aquinas
- Pope Benedict XVI (Joseph Ratzinger)
- Scott Hahn
- Brant Pitre
- Jimmy Akin
- Ludwig Ott
- other respected Catholic scholars

These scholars may be extremely valuable and, in some cases, highly influential. They help interpret sources, organize arguments, provide synthesis, and defend Catholic teaching in accessible forms. However, they do not possess magisterial authority merely by being learned, persuasive, or widely read.

Their work should support understanding of higher authorities, not replace them.

### Level 6 — Academic Scholarship

This level includes broader scholarly work from academic disciplines relevant to Catholic apologetics and theology.

It includes:

- biblical scholarship
- Church history
- Ancient Near Eastern studies
- archaeology
- ancient languages
- classical studies

Academic scholarship is often very valuable for context, method, evidence, chronology, philology, and historical analysis. It can sharpen arguments, expose weak claims, and strengthen careful interpretation. However, it must always be evaluated critically and must never override higher authorities.

Its usefulness depends on the quality of the scholarship, the fairness of its method, and its suitability for the claim being made.

### Level 7 — Reference Works

This level includes tools designed to assist orientation, summary, lookup, terminology, and background research.

It includes:

- *Catholic Bible Dictionary*
- *Catholic Encyclopedia*
- New Advent
- lexicons
- encyclopedias
- atlases
- study Bibles

These are useful reference tools, but they should generally not be cited as the highest authority for theological claims. They are often excellent starting points, quick-reference tools, or aids for locating stronger sources. Their role is supportive rather than controlling.

Reference works may summarize or guide research, but contributors should move from them to primary or higher-level authorities whenever practical.

## Primary Source Principle

Whenever possible, contributors should cite the earliest and highest-authority source available.

Examples:

- If Scripture directly addresses the topic, begin with Scripture.
- If an Ecumenical Council defines a doctrine, cite the Council before modern commentators.
- If a Church Father is being discussed, quote the Father directly before citing modern scholarship about him.

Modern scholarship should explain primary sources rather than replace them.

This principle protects clarity, transparency, and intellectual integrity. It keeps Catholic Veritas grounded in the strongest sources instead of allowing summaries, opinions, or tertiary explanations to become substitutes for direct evidence.

## Resolving Apparent Conflicts

Contributors must handle apparent conflicts carefully and methodically.

If lower-level sources appear to conflict with higher-level authorities, contributors should:

- verify the interpretation
- examine context
- distinguish doctrine from opinion
- distinguish history from theology

Higher authority always prevails.

Lower-level scholarship must never be presented as overriding the Deposit of Faith or authentic Catholic teaching. Apparent conflicts often arise from selective quotation, interpretive confusion, ambiguity about genre, misunderstanding of doctrinal level, or failure to distinguish disciplinary practice from doctrine.

Before describing any real disagreement, contributors should make sure they are not merely comparing unlike things.

## Citation Philosophy

Citations should strengthen transparency, accountability, and reader trust.

Whenever practical, contributors should:

- cite primary sources first
- cite official Church documents directly
- identify Church Fathers by work and approximate date
- distinguish quotations from summaries
- avoid unattributed claims

Citations should make clear what is being quoted, what is being summarized, and what level of authority the source carries. The purpose of citation is not to create the appearance of scholarship, but to let readers see the basis of the claim being made.

## Research Standards

Contributors should:

- distinguish doctrine from theological opinion
- distinguish historical consensus from historical speculation
- distinguish interpretation from established fact
- distinguish official teaching from private opinion

Uncertainty should be stated honestly whenever appropriate.

Contributors must never:

- fabricate sources
- fabricate quotations
- invent historical consensus

Research in Catholic Veritas must be careful, transparent, and proportionate to the claim being made. Strong claims require strong sourcing. Disputed questions require clear framing. Theological confidence must not become an excuse for sloppy historical method, and historical method must not be used to flatten doctrinal distinctions.

## Decision Checklist

Before using any source, contributors should ask:

- Is this the highest available authority?
- Can I cite a primary source instead?
- Am I presenting doctrine or opinion?
- Am I distinguishing history from theology?
- Is this source appropriate for the claim being made?
- Would a stronger source improve this section?

These questions should govern source selection, not merely source collection.

## Long-Term Philosophy

Catholic Veritas seeks to become one of the most trustworthy Catholic apologetics knowledge bases by grounding every article in the strongest available evidence and by giving priority to Divine Revelation, the authentic teaching of the Church, and primary historical sources.

The long-term strength of the project will depend not only on what it says, but on what it builds upon. A durable Catholic knowledge base must rest on a disciplined hierarchy of authority, responsible citation, and a consistent preference for the strongest available sources.
